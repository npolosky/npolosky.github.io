About Me

I am currently studying computer science as an MS student at University of Massachusetts at Amherst and working part time as an Associate Computer Scientist/Engineer in the Marconi-Rosenblatt AI/ML Innovation Lab at ANDRO Computational Solutions LLC, a defense contracting firm in Rome, NY. In May of 2017 I graduated from Cornell University with a BS in Computer Science and worked full time at ANDRO for about two and a half years before beginning at UMass. At work, I have been a contributor on projects involving automatic modulation classification and multi-task learning for signal intelligence; and I am currently the Technical Lead on a project employing reinforcement learning for autonomous UAVs. At school, I have done research through an Independent Study with Dr. Shlomo Zilberstein on safe AI methods for avoiding dead ends in online planning and RL. Currently, I am working with Professors Ina Fiterau & Bruno Castro da Silva on a Master's project involving the use of constrained optimization for safe offline reinforcement learning.

Projects

Deep Reinforcement Learning based Autonomous Unmanned Aeriel Vehicles

At ANDRO, our team was recently awarded the opportunity to conduct research towards building an autonomous UAV for the Navy. In our proposed approach, we intend to use vision-based, deep reinforcement learning agents to aid in navigating a UAV through tactical environments in GPS denied or lost data link scenarios. Additionally, to enhance human-machine teaming, we hope to develop and deploy vision-based natural user interfaces (NUIs) which will allow humans to interface with the UAV via gestures and chromatic symbols.

Intelligent Signal Detector and Classifer

At ANDRO, our team is in the process of devloping a machine learning based RF signal detector and classifier for the Army. The envisioned finished final product should aid EM spectrum analysts in signal identification by providing a multitude of estimated signal characteristics to the user simultaneously. Our approach builds off of our automatic modulation classification (AMC) technology by extending the solution to a multi-task learning paradigm.

Online Probabilistic Planning with Dead Ends

Through an Independent Study at UMass with Dr. Shlomo Zilberstein, I am conducting research on how to enable agents to plan, in real-time, a course of action which avoids exhibiting unsafe behavior. We are particularly concerned with problems in which unsafe behavior may be represented as a time-varying set of state-action pairs which lead to dead-ends. This characterization allows for complex problem domains in which an action that is admissible in a given state at the current time may not be admissible in the same state at a future time.
